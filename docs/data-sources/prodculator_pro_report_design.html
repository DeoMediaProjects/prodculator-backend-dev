Summary Narrative

With a £30M budget and a script that lives overwhelmingly in Lagos interiors — offices, a police station, living rooms and Fuji music venues account for 79% of scenes — the optimal structure anchors production in the United Kingdom under AVEC. That pairs a ~£5.74M net rebate with world-class post-production for a music-heavy drama.

Hungary is the strongest secondary territory: a 30% cash rebate with no cultural test, and Korda/Origo set-build capability suited to an interior-heavy shoot. Malta offers the highest rate of any ranked territory — a 40% cash rebate — on a smaller allocation, with interim payments improving cash flow.

Nigeria offers no formal incentive programme. The case for a Lagos unit is authenticity and currency advantage, not rebate value — it is treated honestly and separately in Section 03.

Taken together: anchor in the UK, build interiors in Hungary or Malta, and capture Lagos on location — the structure that follows this script rather than fighting it.

Story understanding Narrative

Eko Vibes is a music-industry drama that moves between the Fuji venues and police stations of Lagos and the boardrooms of London — a story about ambition negotiated across two cities that treats Lagos not as backdrop but as the production's centre of gravity. The tone is vibrant and celebratory rather than gritty; its urban texture comes from crowds, performance and street life, which shapes everything about how it needs to be shot.

As a production, this is an interior-led shoot with demanding exceptions. Four-fifths of the script plays indoors — offices, living rooms, a police station, music venues — which favours set-build and studio work and reduces weather exposure. But its signature sequences are the opposite: live Fuji performances with large crowds, and night exteriors that account for nearly a quarter of scenes. The music is structural, not incidental, which puts licensing and performance rights on the critical path alongside locations.

Production challenges
  • 28 night scenes — turnaround management and lighting budget in short-daylight territories
  • Live music performance sequences — licensing counsel needed before pre-production
  • Six large-crowd scenes — extras logistics and security, particularly in Lagos
  • Police station access — permits with long lead times in every candidate territory

How to read this chart
1 · Total budget — the full cost of the production, before any incentive.
2 · Gross rebate — the headline 34% applies only to qualifying spend: the £22.5M of the budget the programme counts, not all £30M.
3 · Tax treatment — the UK pays this credit through the tax system, so part is taxed back. That is why the usable rate is 25.5%, not 34%.
4 · Net budget — what the production effectively costs once the £5.74M that actually lands is deducted.

Cash-rebate territories such as Hungary and Malta have no tax-treatment step — their gross and net rates are the same, and their charts show one fewer bar.
